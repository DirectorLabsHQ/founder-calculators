Burn Multiple: When It’s Safe (and When It Kills You)

Burn Multiple tells you how much you're burning to generate each new dollar of ARR. It's brutally honest — and most founders ignore it until it's too late.

Written by operators who’ve watched high burn multiples quietly destroy promising companies.

What Burn Multiple actually measures

Burn Multiple = Net Burn ÷ Net New ARR (over a given period, usually 12 months)

Example: - You burn $2M net cash in a year - You add $1M net new ARR - Burn Multiple = **2.0x**

In simple terms: how many dollars are you willing to lose today to buy $1 of future ARR?

The real story behind the magic numbers

The Burn Multiple benchmarks everyone quotes come from David Sacks (Craft Ventures) and have held up surprisingly well into 2026.

  • ≤1.0x: Elite — you're adding ARR while spending less than you earn. Rare outside mature companies.
  • 1.0–1.5x: Very healthy — efficient growth, attractive to investors.
  • 1.5–2.0x: Acceptable for growth-stage — common in Series A/B.
  • 2.0–3.0x: Caution — you're still growing, but efficiency is slipping.
  • >3.0x: Dangerous — unless you're pre-revenue or in hyper-growth mode with massive funding, this often leads to down-rounds or death.

Stage-Specific Burn Multiple Reality Check (2026)

Pre-$1M ARR (Early / Seed Stage)

  • Acceptable: **2.5–4.0x**
  • Danger: **>4.0x** (unless massive funding or pre-revenue)

High multiples are tolerated here because the focus is on proving product-market fit, not efficiency.

$1M–$10M ARR (Series A/B)

  • Healthy: **1.5–2.5x**
  • Caution: **2.5–3.5x**
  • Danger: **>3.5x**

Investors expect efficiency to improve. Above 3x for multiple quarters often blocks next round or forces cost cuts.

$10M+ ARR (Growth / Late Stage)

  • Strong: **<1.5x**
  • Healthy: **1.5–2.0x**
  • Danger: **>2.5x**

At this scale, high burn multiples signal poor unit economics or over-expansion. Repricing, churn reduction, or restructuring usually follows.

A quick note on churn interaction

Long payback is especially dangerous when churn is high, because customers may leave before you ever recover CAC. Always cross-check payback with your churn rate and net retention.
