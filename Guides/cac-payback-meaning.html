CAC Payback Period: Why 12 Months Is the Magic Number

Investors ask about this metric more than almost anything else. Here's why it matters — and what to do when it's too long.

Built by people who’ve had to defend long paybacks in pitch meetings (and fix them fast).

What CAC Payback actually measures

CAC Payback Period is simple: how many months does it take for a customer to generate enough gross profit to recover the cost of acquiring them?

Payback (months) = CAC ÷ Monthly Gross Profit per Customer

Example: $1,200 CAC ÷ $100 monthly gross profit = 12 months payback.

Why 12 months became the magic number

In the current environment, 12 months is the unofficial benchmark most VCs and growth investors use. Here's why it stuck:

  • Cash cycle efficiency
    If you recover CAC in <12 months, you can reinvest profits into more acquisition before running out of runway. Faster payback = faster compounding.
  • Investor psychology
    12 months feels "annual" — easy to benchmark against yearly budgets and OKRs. Longer than 18 months starts feeling like "venture philanthropy."
  • Market reality
    In high-growth SaaS, gross margins are often 70–85%, ARPU is $100–$300/month — so 12 months aligns with realistic economics.

Realistic CAC Payback Benchmarks (2026)

Pre-$50k MRR (Early Stage)

  • Good: <12 months
  • Acceptable: 12–18 months
  • Danger: >18 months

You can survive longer paybacks here because growth can still compound quickly at this stage.

$50k–$250k MRR (Scaling)

  • Good: <10 months
  • Acceptable: 10–15 months
  • Danger: >15 months

Investors start demanding efficiency. >15 months often blocks Series A or makes terms worse.

$250k+ MRR / $1M+ ARR (Mature Growth)

  • Good: <8 months
  • Acceptable: 8–12 months
  • Danger: >12 months

At this scale, long paybacks kill profitability and make fundraising harder — focus on ARPU and margins first.

A quick note on churn interaction

Long payback is especially dangerous when churn is high, because customers may leave before you ever recover CAC. Always cross-check payback with your churn rate and net retention.
